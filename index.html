Growth Strategy Consulting

Cellfield

A market expansion project: making the case for Cellfield to reach university students with dyslexia.

ClientCellfield Reading Matters UK
TypeMarket Expansion Consulting
ResearchPrimary & Secondary
Year2025
01 Overview

Cellfield Reading Matters UK Ltd holds the UK licensing rights for a patented reading intervention program designed for people with dyslexia. The program had built a solid track record with its traditional audience of children aged 8 to 12. The question put to our team was whether university students with dyslexia represented a credible new market, and if so, how Cellfield should approach it.

The five-person team project combined primary research (a survey across five universities and one-to-one interviews with students) with secondary research mapping potential university partners and scrutinizing the program's evidence base. An in-person visit to Cellfield's Stirling training center was a turning point in how we framed the recommendations.

Four recommendations were delivered to Cellfield's UK license holder, each grounded in the research and sequenced in the order they need to happen.

02 The Challenge

What Cellfield does

The program forms new neurological pathways through visual and auditory stimulation, targeting the root causes of reading difficulty rather than compensating for them. Training runs over ten days of one-hour supervised sessions, followed by a ten-week home reading phase and a six-month follow-up. Total cost: £1,200.

The business problem

Demand is tied to school term dates, creating a predictable cycle of busy and quiet periods that makes running a Cellfield centre as a standalone business difficult. University students operate on a different calendar entirely. A student pipeline during term time would smooth the revenue curve and open an audience the sector had not seriously pursued.

03 What We Did

Primary research was conducted across five universities in a survey format, targeting students with reading difficulties via disability services, departments, event clubs, societies, and on-campus posters with scannable QR codes. Three in-person interviews were conducted: a parent of a child already enrolled in the Cellfield program, a first-year university student with dyslexia, and a final-year university student with dyslexia.

Secondary Research
Three universities were identified as the most credible first targets: University of Strathclyde (existing Cellfield relationship, strong disability infrastructure, collaborative research program with private businesses), University of Glasgow (identified gaps in staff dyslexia training), and the University of the West of Scotland (strong neurodiversity program Cellfield could integrate into). The evidence base for the program was also examined. It is broadly strong, but published challenges from Macquarie University exist and need to be addressed proactively rather than ignored.

We also made sure to visit the premises. Before the site visit, we assumed the price was the central problem. After trying the training ourselves and understanding what goes into delivering it, the question shifted: the cost is a fair reflection of the service. The real problem is how students access funding.

04 What We Found
0% Students aware of Cellfield before the survey
73.7% Interested after hearing about it clear unprompted demand
£462 Average expected price versus £1,200 actual cost

Zero awareness existed for the Cellfield program among students. However, a strong latent interest emerged after learning about the company and the program procedure, alongside a price expectation of £462 which was less than half the actual cost. Those three findings defined the entire project. The question was never whether the market existed. It was how to make it accessible.

The interviews added texture the survey could not. Both students described years of dyslexia being minimized or mishandled in school, with university being better but still leaving reading gaps that compensatory tools did not close. After being told what Cellfield does, both said they would consider it. The program addresses the underlying problem and not just the symptoms, and that distinction mattered to them.

05 Recommendations

We made four recommendations to Cellfield, each building on the previous one.

01 Get accredited

Pursue external accreditation and peer-reviewed publication to resolve the evidence gap before approaching any institution.

02 Find the funding

Identify charities willing to fund student placements, making the £1,200 cost within reach for the right candidates.

03 Enter university networks

Contact neurodiverse societies and student support services at Strathclyde, Glasgow, and UWS directly.

04 Adapt the program

Redesign scheduling and delivery to fit student timetables, including online sessions and fewer required trips to center.

Why in this order: Accreditation unlocks everything else. No institution will recommend Cellfield to students without confidence in the evidence, and no charity will fund placements without it. Once that foundation is in place, funding solves the cost barrier more effectively than trying to compress the program cost, because the £1,200 reflects the genuine intensity of the delivery and cannot easily be reduced. With accreditation and funding in place, the university outreach becomes a straightforward conversation rather than a cold pitch.

All of these findings and recommendations were delivered and discussed with Cellfield's UK license holder who wishes to remain unnamed.

06 Takeaways

The site visit to Stirling was the most valuable part of this project. Trying the training first-hand shifted the framing from "how do we lower the price" to "how do we help students fund it". That is a fundamentally different problem, and the recommendations would have been weaker without that experience.

The evidence question was more significant than anticipated going in. Cellfield's results are well-supported, but the existence of published challenges means any credible institutional partner will raise it. Getting ahead of that is not optional for the expansion to work at scale.

Diversity & Inclusion Consulting

Morgan Stanley D&I

A strategic audit of Morgan Stanley's diversity and inclusion position, with practical recommendations for improvement.

ClientMorgan Stanley
TypeD&I Audit & Consulting
FrameworksSTIRDEEPER, SWOT, VRIO, GAP
Year2025
01 Overview

This was a five-person team consulting project focused on Morgan Stanley's diversity and inclusion practices. The brief was to assess where the firm stood and to produce grounded, practical recommendations based on the analysis.

Four recommendations came out of the work, each backed by specific evidence rather than generic best-practice advice. The deliverable was a written report and a presentation delivered to members of Morgan Stanley.

02 Why This Matters

Morgan Stanley at a glance

One of the world's largest investment banks, operating across institutional securities, wealth management, and investment management. As a major employer in financial services across the US and UK, its D&I practices carry weight both internally for the people who work there, and externally as a signal to clients, candidates, and regulators.

The specific pressure

Financial services is one of the least diverse sectors at senior levels. In their 2021 Diversity and Inclusion report, Morgan Stanley made a public commitment to reach 40% diversity in leadership roles by 2024. The gap between that commitment and the actual numbers in its own reports was what this project set out to examine.

03 What We Did

The team carried out an audit of Morgan Stanley across its external and internal environment. The analysis used a STIRDEEPER framework for the external environment, and a combination of SWOT, VRIO, and GAP analyses for the internal. Morgan Stanley's 2021 Diversity and Inclusion Report provided the primary source material, alongside independent industry data from Statista and McKinsey.

04 What We Found
Top 5 US banks for racial diversity behind JP Morgan Chase
39% Female workforce vs. 53% industry average
26% Women in senior leadership across US and UK operations

On racial diversity, Morgan Stanley performs well, sitting in the top five US banks. The $12bn investment in closing the racial wealth gap compares favourably against Citi Group's $1bn equivalent. The gender picture, however, is considerably weaker.

The UK financial services sector averages 53% female employees. Morgan Stanley sits at 39%. That 14-percentage-point gap against the industry norm is the most significant finding in the analysis.

SWOT
Strengths: well-established D&I plan, wide range of unconscious bias training.
Weaknesses: senior leadership underrepresentation, no programmes for indigenous or disabled candidates.
Opportunities: widen scholarships to include indigenous and disabled candidates; access more customers and employees through increased diversity.
Threat: uneven parental leave, low female representation in upper management creating an employee retention risk.
VRIO
Most valuable internal D&I assets: Employee Resource Groups (ERGs) and the D&I Council. These are capabilities that take time to build and are not easily replicated. Their value depends entirely on whether they are being actively and systematically used.
GAP
The gap between Morgan Stanley's current position and its stated targets is most visible in leadership diversity. The 40% leadership target for 2024 was publicly committed to, but tracking against it and publishing that tracking transparently was found to be inconsistent.
External
The external environment is moving faster than Morgan Stanley's internal gender progress. Competitors such as JP Morgan (UK) outperform Morgan Stanley on total workforce gender balance. Supplier diversity tracking is absent, an area peers are starting to close.
05 Recommendations
01 Diversify leadership

Set and publish measurable targets for gender and racial representation at Director level and above, tracked quarterly.

02 Expand programs

Extend internship and scholarship schemes to explicitly include indigenous and disabled candidates, two groups consistently excluded from financial services D&I initiatives.

03 Make training measurable

Move from undisclosed to a transparent, published approach combining quantitative uptake data with qualitative outcome surveys.

04 Equalise parental leave

Address the imbalance between maternity and paternity leave, a structural signal about gender roles that directly contradicts the firm's stated D&I commitments.

The commercial argument: McKinsey's research across 366 financial companies found that firms in the top quartile for gender diversity were 15% more likely to achieve above-median financial returns. For racial diversity, that figure was 35%. Leadership diversity is not a values argument for Morgan Stanley; it is a performance argument backed by data from its own sector.

The other three recommendations follow from the first. Expanding programs is an extension of existing infrastructure rather than a rebuild. Making training measurable is straightforward given the employee survey systems already in place. Equalising parental leave is the most operationally simple recommendation and one of the most symbolically significant. Uneven leave policies send a clear signal about the firm's actual priorities regardless of what its reports say.

Brand Repositioning Strategy

Tennent's

"True to You": making Scotland's most iconic lager relevant to the next generation of drinkers without abandoning the heritage that makes it distinctive.

ClientC&C Group / Tennent's
TypeBrand Audit & Repositioning
Audience targetEnthuse Segment · 24–35
Year2025
01 Overview

Tennent's Lager is a household name in Scotland, but local is the operative word. Outside Scotland, the brand barely registers in the national UK market. The brief was to find out why, and then build a repositioning strategy that could change it.

The central argument: Tennent's had been communicating its Scottish identity as something to preserve. The opportunity was to reframe it as something to celebrate in a modern, progressive sense, without abandoning the heritage that makes the brand distinctive.

02 The Challenge

Tennent's competitors, including BrewDog (also Scottish) and Heineken, had found ways to connect with 18- to 35-year-olds through bolder branding, wider product ranges, and active social media strategies. Tennent's had not kept pace. The brand was thriving with its loyal older audience while a younger generation was quietly moving on.

The task was to understand what was holding the brand back and where it could realistically grow, with the Enthuse audience as the primary focus.

The 900,000-person 'Enthuse' segment (24–35-year-olds) had been identified internally as a growth target. None of the brand's current activity was reaching them.

03 What Was Done

The work involved a full brand audit spanning six areas: the brand itself, the product range, the market, the audience, the digital presence, and the competitive landscape. From that foundation, a campaign strategy called "True to You" was developed alongside a 4P execution plan.

04 The Analysis

Understanding the full picture of the brand was essential before any recommendations were made. The analysis ran across five categories.

The Brand
Strong assets: 135-year-old recipe, the red "T" logo, gold-and-copper brand colour, and "Scotland's Favourite Pint" slogan. The brand purpose sits in a product-and-occasion frame that gives younger consumers nothing to connect with emotionally.
The Range
Four products: original lager, Tennent's Tank, Light, and Zero. Offering is thin against competitors offering dozens of formats. Light and Zero are the right products for the Enthuse audience, but they are barely being marketed.
The Market
No and low alcohol grew by 39.7% in 2023. 30% of under-45s now drink no/low regularly. Tennent's already makes the relevant products. The problem is reach, not product range.
The Audience
Protect Audience (35–44, loyal, revenue-generating) vs. Enthuse Audience (24–35, roughly 900,000 people, identified but unconverted). Growth sits entirely in the Enthuse segment.
Digital
Around 64,000 followers across Instagram and X, but engagement is below what that following suggests. TikTok and Snapchat, where the Enthuse audience spends most of its time, are essentially untouched.
The Pattern
Every lens pointed to the same conclusion: strong assets, credible product, and a brand story that has not evolved while a younger audience has quietly moved on.
Core Insight

Tennent's biggest strength had quietly become its biggest weakness. Scottish identity doesn't have to mean conservative identity. Modern Scotland is a progressive country with a proud LGBTQ+ history. Tennent's could legitimately claim that side of Scottish culture too, without abandoning its heritage.

Reaching the 900,000-person Enthuse audience requires Scottishness itself to evolve: from heritage-as-preservation to heritage-as-acceptance.

05 The Strategy

The proposed campaign, True to You, keeps the Scottish identity but reframes it around authenticity, self-expression, and inclusion. Light and Zero Alcohol move to the front as the products best suited to the Enthuse audience. The brand aligns with genuine social causes, specifically supporting the LGBTQ+ community through authentic year-round activism, not just Pride-month marketing. And it finally builds a real digital presence on TikTok and Snapchat, led by younger social-media managers and Scottish influencer partnerships.

The risk was acknowledged openly: the Protect audience might push back on a more progressive brand expression. The solution is a split portfolio. Classic Tennent's lager stays premium and traditional, protecting the Protect audience's revenue. Light and Zero carry the new campaign, recruiting the Enthuse audience. The brand evolves without forcing a choice on its existing base.

A brand that tries to speak to everyone ends up meaning nothing to anyone. True to You takes a deliberate position, which means some pushback is likely. That is the right trade-off to address the growth issues.

06 Execution Pointers

Each element of the marketing mix was directly tied to either protecting the Protect audience or actively recruiting the Enthuse segment.

Product
New flavour variants for Light and Zero aligned to fruit-forward preferences of 18- to 34-year-olds. Redesigned cans. Classic lager recipe untouched.
Price
Modest increase on core lager to reinforce premium repositioning. Light and Zero held at accessible price points to keep trial barriers low.
Place
Free taste tests and activations at university bars and on-trade venues, directly addressing the "older-person's-pint" perception at the point it forms.
Promotion
Premium craft-style visuals for core lager. Values-led content for Light and Zero on TikTok and Snapchat, amplified through Scottish influencer partnerships.
07 Takeaways

The most interesting part of this project was not the tactics, but accepting that good brand strategy often means a real trade-off. A safer recommendation would have polished the existing identity and added a few new flavours. That would not have closed the Enthuse gap. Incremental refreshes do not fix relevance problems.

The stronger course of action takes a position on what Tennent's stands for, which carries real risk with the Protect audience. The split portfolio strategy is what makes that risk manageable: by running two tracks simultaneously, the brand can evolve in one direction while holding firm in another.

Integrated Marketing Communications

PWR-BRU

"PWR-BRU Powers You Through": a full IMC campaign for AG Barr's energy drink.

ClientAG Barr / PWR-BRU
TypeIMC Campaign
Target18–34 year-olds
Year2025
01 Overview

PWR-BRU, AG Barr's energy drink launched under the Irn-Bru parent brand. Despite the backing of one of Scotland's most culturally embedded soft drinks brands, PWR-BRU had almost no marketing presence at the time. No campaign. No meaningful social following. A product on shelves with no story attached to it.

The campaign proposed was called My PWR Day, which was a month-long contest combining social media, outdoor advertising, in-store activation, and audio and streaming placements. It was designed to introduce PWR-BRU to 18- to 34-year-olds who had never been given a reason to try it, and to tackle the stigma that surrounds energy drink consumption in the process.

02 Context: Market & Consumer

The Competition

Red Bull, Monster, and Lucozade dominate the national market. Each is backed by decades of sponsorship in sport, music, and culture. Red Bull owns extreme sports. Monster owns underground music and gaming. Lucozade owns athletic performance. PWR-BRU had none of that brand territory staked out.

The Consumer

18- to 34-year-olds. Over 1.3 million in the UK drink two to three energy drinks per week. Their motivations are practical (energy, alertness, reduced fatigue), but their brand choices are emotional. They buy what feels like them, what they can connect with. They also respond to brands that give them something to participate in rather than just observe.

PWR-BRU had strong parent-brand equity and a ready-made consumer segment. The product existed. The audience existed. Neither knew about the other.

03 The Challenge
1.3m+ Weekly energy drink consumers in the UK aged 18 to 34
Zero Active PWR-BRU campaigns at the time of this project
£0 Estimated social media spend before this campaign

This project's opportunity was not to reinvent the brand but to switch it on. The Irn-Bru heritage gave PWR-BRU a personality to draw on: bold, irreverent, unapologetically Scottish. None of that was being used.

04 What We Did

Our five-person team's task was to identify a real communications opportunity for PWR-BRU, then design and present a complete integrated marketing communications plan using at least one digital and one traditional channel. The approach involved a situational analysis covering the competitive market and the target consumer, followed by audience definition, SMART objective setting, and a full campaign strategy and media plan built around the My PWR Day concept.

05 The Audience

Primary: 18- to 34-year-olds with active, busy lifestyles, including students, young professionals, gym-goers, and commuters who consume energy drinks as part of a daily routine. Their media usage centres on TikTok, Instagram, Spotify, YouTube, and Twitch. They are sceptical of advertising that feels corporate, but highly responsive to campaigns that give them something to do or be part of.

Secondary: Existing Irn-Bru consumers. A lower-effort conversion as they needed to know PWR-BRU existed and that it carried the same values they already associated with the parent brand.

06 Campaign Strategy

Five measurable objectives were defined for the one-month campaign duration.

Objective How it's measured
1 Raise product awareness by at least 20% within two months of launch. Pre- and post-campaign survey tracking prompted and unprompted awareness.
2 Increase supermarket sales by at least 10% per stocked location within one month. Sales data comparison by store before and after campaign goes live.
3 Grow PWR-BRU social following by 100,000 during the campaign month. Follower tracking across all active platforms throughout the campaign.
4 Place one off-fixture display in every major supermarket before launch. Store confirmation log tracking which displays are live and where they were located.
5 Generate 1 million hashtag uses by the end of the campaign month. Hashtag monitoring across Instagram, TikTok, and X.
07 Execution: Campaign & Tactics

My PWR Day: a month-long contest built around one question: how does PWR-BRU get you through your day? Participants posted short video reels on any platform, tagged with the campaign hashtag. Two incentive tiers drove participation.

The Prizes

Top 10 most-liked reels received a free three-month supply of PWR-BRU. The overall winner got to design their own PWR-BRU flavour and can artwork as a limited-edition release. That second incentive created a story worth sharing, not just a free product giveaway.

The Reframe

Energy drinks carry a stigma, commonly associated with excess and poor lifestyle choices. My PWR Day deliberately showed the drink as part of a productive, active, ordinary day. That reframe was built into the creative brief from the start.

Social Media
TikTok and Instagram as the campaign engine, with daily posting and creator partnerships to seed early entries. Platform-first content designed to look native, not advertised.
Audio
Spotify and YouTube Music ads reaching the audience during commutes and workouts, the exact moments PWR-BRU is relevant.
OOH
Outdoor billboards and posters covering high-footfall urban locations. TV and Twitch ads provided broader reach.
In-Store
Off-fixture displays live before launch, ensuring the first in-store encounter looked like a brand already in motion.
08 Takeaways

My PWR Day can work as a concept because it gave consumers a genuine reason to participate. The limited-edition flavour prize in particular generated strong discussion about what meaningful brand engagement looks like, as opposed to hashtag activity that produces numbers without producing loyalty.

Since access to internal data was restricted, some slides in the presentation used structural labels rather than worked-through insight, and a stronger plan would have included specific data on PWR-BRU's distribution footprint, and a more detailed breakdown of how each competitor allocates its communications spend.

International Market Entry Plan

Wheyl

An internationalisation plan for a Philippines-based supplement startup entering the Malaysian market on a limited budget and a low-risk entry model.

ClientWheyl Nutrition Co.
TypeInternationalisation Plan
MarketMalaysia
Year2024
01 Overview

This project was an internationalisation plan for Wheyl Nutrition Co., a supplement startup based in the Philippines. The task was to identify the right foreign market for their flagship product, JUST Whey Protein, and build a complete entry plan covering market selection, cultural and legal considerations, pricing, promotion, distribution and the people needed to execute it. The key constraint throughout was that Wheyl is a startup with limited resources, so the selected market had to be low-risk and accessible rather than simply the largest available.

02 Context: The Company & Product

Wheyl Nutrition Co. was founded in 2017 in the Philippines as a lifestyle-focused supplement startup selling whey protein, plant-based protein, pre-workouts, creatine, electrolytes and BCAAs. The brand positions itself against the typical supplement category by using inclusive imagery, neutral colours and friendly language rather than the aggressive aesthetics that most competitors rely on.

The Product

The product chosen for this plan is JUST Whey Protein, Wheyl's flagship. Selected for its versatility as a pre/post-workout drink, meal replacement or daily supplement, and because it is easy to adapt in terms of size, packaging and flavour for different markets.

Domestic User Profile

Core consumer: urban, Philippines, aged 18 to 30, middle-class income, 51 to 49 male-to-female ratio. Motivations include healthier eating and active lifestyles. Key fears are around product safety, effectiveness, misleading claims and cost.

03 The Market Opportunity
$25.5bn Global protein supplement market current value (Euromonitor, 2024)
$37.1bn Projected value within 5 years strong growth trajectory
41.4% North American market share of total global revenue (2022)

The global market is growing, but not all of it is accessible from a standing start. Four regions were assessed: North America and Europe are mature and saturated, the Middle East lacks enough category demand, and Asia offered the most practical entry point given proximity to the Philippines and lower barriers for a startup with limited capital.

North America
Ruled out. 41.4% of global revenue but saturated, dominated by established brands, and expensive to ship to from the Philippines.
Europe
Ruled out. Similarly mature, with a 25 to 50 consumer base and strong local competition. Distance adds further cost.
Middle East
Ruled out. Growing but insufficient category demand for a company at Wheyl's stage.
Asia
Selected. Consumers aged 20 to 35, rising middle-class income and geographic proximity to the Philippines reduces cost and complexity.
Internationalisation Rationale

Wheyl needs a market that does not require large upfront investment and carries minimal risk. The focus is on finding somewhere accessible to test, not the largest opportunity available.

04 Choosing the Market

Using Cavusgil's (2020) five-step indexing approach, three Asian markets were shortlisted across seven indicators: population, consumer preferences, competition, market intensity, growth, consumer value, and infrastructure.

Ruled Out
Indonesia
Underdeveloped e-commerce infrastructure. Wheyl's digital-first model needs a mature online retail ecosystem to work.
Ruled Out
Singapore
High competitive density and a small addressable market. Cutting through would cost more than Wheyl can commit at this stage.
Selected
Malaysia
Median age 26, mature e-commerce on Lazada and Shopee, less competition than Singapore, and cultural scores very close to the Philippines.
Population & Age
Median age of 26, with urban consumers skewing younger than both Indonesia and Singapore. Aligns well with Wheyl's 18 to 30 target.
E-Commerce
Mature ecosystem built around Lazada and Shopee, the same type of platforms Wheyl already uses domestically. No new infrastructure needed.
Competition
Less competitive than Singapore. Optimum Nutrition and Myprotein are present but the market is not saturated, leaving room for a new entrant.
Cultural Fit
Very close Hofstede scores to the Philippines, meaning the brand tone, content approach and messaging carry over with only minor changes.
05 Cultural and Legal Landscape

Hofstede's six dimensions were used to compare the Philippines and Malaysia. The countries score closely overall, but three dimensions have specific implications for how Wheyl enters and communicates.

The Halal Advantage

63.5% of Malaysia's population is Muslim, so Halal certification is required for consumer products. Wheyl's products are already Halal certified in the Philippines, which speeds up the JAKIM certification process most international brands have to complete from scratch.

Language & Labelling

English is widely used in Malaysian business and education, so existing labelling works as a starting point. Adding Malay elements to packaging over time would help with local market appeal, but no full redesign is needed at entry.

Marketing & Distribution
Health claims must follow Ministry of Health guidelines and be factual. Malaysian Halal logo required on packaging.
E-Commerce & Data
Full compliance with Malaysia's PDPA data protection act required for all e-commerce operations.
Foreign Investment
No restrictions for Wheyl. Government investment incentives available for sustainable and Halal-certified products, both of which Wheyl qualifies for.
Environmental Laws
Wheyl's existing biodegradable packaging already satisfies Malaysian sustainability requirements. No changes needed.
Employment Law
Employment Act 1955 and Industrial Relations Act 1967 govern all hires. Expatriate staff are on the same terms as local hires.
06 Execution: The Entry Plan

Standardised

Product ingredients, formulations and existing flavour range stay unchanged. Biodegradable packaging material carries over as it already meets Malaysian sustainability law. Brand tone and social media approach translate directly.

Adapted

A 1kg pack size is introduced as the Malaysian platform standard. The JAKIM Halal logo is added via sticker on packaging. A locally inspired flavour variant is developed to build relevance with Malaysian consumers.

Pricing

A cost-plus strategy covers additional costs (manufacturing, transport, Halal certification and distribution), with prices benchmarked against competitor listings on Malaysian platforms.

Promotion & Entry Mode

Wheyl already operates on TikTok, Facebook and Instagram, which Statista (2024) confirms are the top three platforms in Malaysia. The community-led, user-generated content approach carries over without overhaul. Given Malaysia scores slightly higher on individualism, messaging can shift slightly toward personal product benefits. Local influencers are brought in as independent contractors managed by the Malaysian social media hire.

E-Commerce
Sales through Wheyl's own website and listings on Lazada and Shopee, mirroring the domestic model. No new infrastructure needed to start.
Indirect Exporting
As brand recognition grows, products are exported to supplement stores, gyms and department stores. Adds physical presence without requiring a full local operation upfront.

Staffing requirements differ by entry mode. E-commerce only needs one social media manager. The combined model also needs an operations manager. Both are full-time roles under Malaysian employment law.

08 Takeaways
01 Malaysia is the right first market

Passing on North America and Europe was correct. The costs and competition would have been too much at this stage. Malaysia was chosen because it was the most accessible option, not the biggest.

02 Existing certifications reduce the compliance burden

Wheyl's Halal certification and biodegradable packaging already satisfy two key Malaysian requirements that most international brands have to build from scratch before entering.

03 Hire locally for social, send internally for operations

The social media role needs someone based in Malaysia with real knowledge of the market and language. The operations role is better filled by an existing Wheyl employee who already understands the business.

04 Phase 2 is still an open question

E-commerce and indirect export is a sensible starting point, but limits how fast physical presence builds. Local distribution partnerships or a retail push in Kuala Lumpur is the likely next move if entry performs well.

Market Selection Logic

Every part of this plan connects back to choosing Malaysia. Geographic proximity, cultural similarity, a ready e-commerce infrastructure, Halal certification alignment and a young consumer base with less competition than Singapore. That one decision shaped everything else.

Rubicon: Fru-Tea

01 Overview

This project was a full product launch plan for AG Barr plc, the company behind Irn-Bru, Rubicon, and a broad portfolio of UK soft drinks. The brief was to identify a credible growth opportunity within the existing brand portfolio and build a complete go-to-market plan for a new product aimed at Gen Z consumers, ready for a September 2025 launch.

02 The Challenge

Identifying a credible opportunity meant first understanding what was changing in the market, and the change was not just about sugar reduction. It was about expectations. Gen Z expects drinks to feel lighter, more natural, and aligned with healthier lifestyles, without compromising on taste. They want a drink they can enjoy without feeling guilty.

Two behaviours sit at the centre of this audience, and neither is currently served well by any brand in the AG Barr portfolio. 38% of Gen Z actively prioritise health in their soft-drink purchases, and 74% seek interesting flavours when they buy. Traditional RTD tea addresses health but concedes flavour excitement. Mainstream carbonated soft drinks deliver flavour but carry sugar and synthetic baggage. Nothing in the market was balancing that trade-off.

Gen Z does not want to choose between healthy and exciting. The winning product refuses the choice: natural, functional, and flavour-led in a format Gen Z actually drinks.

Research into the RTD tea category put numbers behind the problem. A £86m market growing at 2.67% annually, with the carbonated sub-segment accelerating at 11% year-on-year, confirmed this was not a niche to observe from a distance. Retailers like Tesco were already responding, dedicating standalone functional bays to the category. The market was moving whether AG Barr entered it or not.

A competitive audit of the AG Barr portfolio confirmed that only one brand could credibly carry a tea innovation: Rubicon, with its fruit-forward identity and multicultural Gen Z audience. The gap in the market and the gap in the portfolio pointed to the same answer.

04 The Approach

The next decision was what to build on top of that brand. A plain or botanical RTD tea was off the table. Rubicon's equity is bold, exotic and fruit-led, and the audience that buys it won't reach for something quiet. The product had to be sparkling, fruit-forward, and visually distinct from the flat bottles that dominate the category.

Product decisions followed from that brief. Liquid was specified mid-tier, costly enough to carry real fruit juice without breaking the margin and cheap enough to leave room for marketing investment. Packaging was full-colour with a tactile finish and coloured tabs, designed to be impossible to miss on a chilled shelf. The supply chain was built inside AG Barr's existing infrastructure, with green tea sourced from established estates and filling handled through current manufacturing. No new fixed costs, no new vendor risk.

The output was a ten-slide board-facing launch plan and a three-year financial model, unified by a single campaign idea: "Out with the Old, In with the Cold." That line was designed to carry the same meaning whether someone saw it on a shelf talker, a social post, or a sampling activation.

05 The Strategy

Four decisions defined the launch. Everything downstream, including the retailer pitch, the in-store activation, and the three-year financial model, was built to execute against them.

Audience

06 Execution: Route to Market

With the launch defined, the next test was whether a retailer would stock it. Getting a new product onto a shelf is not a given. Retailers have finite space, established relationships, and very little patience for a pitch that does not immediately justify the risk of a listing. The route to market plan was built around one question: why would a buyer say yes?

The case for the retailer

Five arguments were built into the customer proposition, each designed to address a different concern a buyer would raise.

The margin decision

A proposed margin of 40% profit on return was set for the customer, matching the category average exactly. The logic was deliberate. Going above 40% would signal that Rubicon was trying to buy a listing rather than earn one, which erodes credibility. Going below would have created friction with buyers who have category-wide margin expectations to protect. Matching the average removes both risks and keeps the negotiation focused on product and brand rather than price.

Winning in store: the shopper journey

Getting a listing is only half the problem. Once Fru-Tea is on the shelf, it needs to be seen, chosen, and repurchased. The route to market plan addressed this across two distinct moments.

The First Moment of Truth is the split second a shopper stands in front of the fixture and decides what to pick up. Fru-Tea was designed to win here through three things: packaging that stands out in a chilled bay dominated by flat matte formats, POS material at shelf level to direct attention, and strategic positioning within the fixture near the functional drinks bay rather than the commodity soft drinks section.

The Second Moment of Truth is the first sip. A product can win the shelf and lose the repeat purchase if the taste does not match the promise on the label. A live sampling programme was built into the launch plan to close this gap. Getting product into the hands of a Gen Z shopper at the point of decision removes the risk of trial and converts curiosity into a first purchase with significantly higher conversion than POS alone.

07The Numbers

All figures below are projected estimates based on modelled assumptions. They represent a three-year financial scenario built for a board-facing launch proposal, not audited results.

Every number in this model starts from a single decision: what does a bottle of Fru-Tea cost the person buying it? The answer is £1.49. That is the recommended retail price, set to sit comfortably within the everyday soft drink tier while signalling something a step above own-label. Everything else in the financial model flows from that figure.

Step 1. What the retailer earns and what we receive

Retailers in this category work on a standard 40% profit margin. Once VAT is removed from the shelf price and the retailer takes their share, Rubicon receives £8.94 per case of 12 bottles. That is the Invoiced Revenue per case, the figure from which every cost and investment is then deducted.

The volume projections below are what drive total Invoiced Revenue across the three years. These assumptions are built on a realistic store rollout: starting with 5,000 stores in Year 1, growing to 10,000 in Year 2, and reaching 15,000 in Year 3, with weekly sales per store increasing as brand awareness builds.

Step 2. What it costs to make each bottle

The cost of producing a case of 12 bottles is £5.16. That breaks down into three parts: the liquid inside each bottle (fruit juice, green tea, natural flavours) costs £3.60 per case, the packaging (glass bottle, label, shelf-ready outer) costs 96p per case, and the cost to manufacture and fill each bottle adds a further 60p per case. Fru-Tea uses no added sugar, so there is no Soft Drinks Industry Levy to account for.

Once the cost of goods is subtracted from what the retailer pays, the remaining margin is called the Invoice Contribution. At £3.78 per case (42%), this is a healthy starting position. It gives the brand room to fund the investment needed to launch.

Step 3. What it costs to launch and grow

Launching a new product is expensive. Three separate pools of investment are required, each expressed as a percentage of Invoiced Revenue. These step down deliberately over time as the brand becomes established and needs less support to sustain its position.

Reading the result

Year 1 projects a loss of £0.76m. This is not a failure of the plan; it is the plan. Getting into 5,000 stores, funding shelf visibility, and building brand awareness from zero requires significant upfront spend. The model accounts for this honestly rather than hiding it.

By Year 2, the brand turns profitable at a projected +£0.38m. The investment spend has decreased significantly while the volume more than triples. By Year 3, the Net Contribution reaches +£2.43m as the brand matures, investment costs fall, and scale begins to work in its favour.

Across all three years combined, the plan projects a positive Net Contribution of +£2.05m, meeting the board's requirement that new product launches reach payback within a three-year window. Two risks were named in the submission: competitors cutting shelf prices or running promotions in response to a new listing, and Year 2 distribution rollout taking longer than projected if early sell-through data does not convince buyers to expand the footprint on schedule. Both were treated as manageable rather than fatal, and neither changes the three-year payback outcome under reasonable stress-test assumptions.
