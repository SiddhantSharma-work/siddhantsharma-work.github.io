Brand Strategy · Repositioning

Tennent's Lager: a 'True to You' repositioning for Scotland's oldest beer brand.

A brand audit and one-year repositioning plan, reframing a 450-year-old Scottish institution for a new generation of drinkers, without abandoning the cultural equity that built it.

ClientC&C Group
BrandTennent's Lager
ScopeBrand audit & repositioning
Plan horizonApr 2025 / Mar 2026
01 Context

A 450-year brand facing a category it no longer leads.

Tennent's Lager is Scotland's most recognisable beer, brewed at the Wellpark Brewery in Glasgow since 1885 and registered under its iconic red 'T' trademark in 1876. Its brand essence, articulated internally as "Scotland in a can," has driven decades of dominant category salience in the home market.

The deliverable was a board-facing strategic recommendation grounded in brand audit, secondary market research, and marketing-mix in order for the brand compete effectively in its respective market.

02 Problem

High salience, low meaning. Dominant locally, losing ground nationally.

The brand audit surfaced a structural tension. Tennent's brand assets, the red T, the copper-gold colourway, the "Scotland's Favourite Pint" slogan, remain among the strongest in UK lager. But brand purpose sits in a product-and-occasion frame (quality beer, collective celebration) that offers consumers little to identify with, in the sense Stokburger-Sauer et al. (2012) argue drives consumer-brand identification. Identity is loud; meaning is quiet.

That translates directly into audience risk. Tennent's internal segmentation splits the market into two cohorts: a 'Protect' audience (male, 35 to 44, loyal, revenue-defending) and an 'Enthuse' audience (24 to 35, circa 900,000 people, identified but unconverted). The Enthuse group lives on TikTok and Snapchat, platforms Tennent's does not occupy, and remains unsold on the taste and quality of the core lager.

The category itself is moving where the brand isn't. The no/low alcohol segment grew 39.7% in 2023, 30% of under-45s now drink no/low, and consumers are increasingly willing to pay a premium for ingredient credibility. Tennent's Light and Zero sit inside that growth wave but under-communicate for it, and the brand's 64,000 combined followers across Instagram and X meaningfully under-index on engagement.

Insight

Tennent's conservatism isn't protecting the brand, it's quietly shrinking it. Scottish identity is the moat, but the version of 'Scottish' the brand sells was written for a generation ageing out. To reach the 900,000 Enthuse drinkers, Scottishness itself has to evolve: from heritage-as-preservation to heritage-as-acceptance.

04 Strategy

Reposition Scotland's meaning, not its assets.

The strategy keeps the brand's distinctive assets intact (the red T, the colourway, the Scottish codes) and reframes what they mean. A new campaign platform, True to You, leans into Scotland's LGBTQ+ history and contemporary identity, presenting Scottishness as living and inclusive rather than preserved. It is a meaning-shift, not an identity-shift, designed to recruit the Enthuse cohort while maintaining brand equity with the Protect core.

Positioning
Tennent's is the fabric brand of Scotland, built on pride and heritage while embracing the country's ever-changing landscape.
Target
The 'Enthuse' segment, 18 to 35, circa 900,000 people, spanning genders and sub-generations.
Mission
Recruit 100,000 new Tennent's drinkers domestically across the UK within the plan horizon.

02.

07 Impact

Recruit the next generation, premiumise the core.

  • Deliver sampling and trial activations across university bars and on-trade venues to convert the Enthuse cohort.
  • Launch True to You on TikTok and Snapchat under a refreshed social-media function.
Long-horizon targets
  • Grow social reach to 50K on Instagram and 50K on X, with matched presence on TikTok and Snapchat.
  • Reposition the core lager in consumer perception as premium yet affordable, a credible trade-up within the category.
  • Deliver the 100,000 new domestic drinker mission within the plan horizon.
08 Risk

The named trade-off the plan has to manage.

The True to You platform carries a real positioning trade-off. Leaning into Scotland's LGBTQ+ identity speaks directly to the Enthuse audience but risks alienating elements of the Protect core, a primarily older male cohort that may hold more conservative views on the framing. Loyalty lost here translates directly into revenue lost, and the same cohort is already under pressure from Minimum Unit Pricing, inflation, and tightening alcohol-marketing regulation.

How the plan manages it

Phased execution, Light and Zero carry the activism platform; the core lager carries the premiumisation story. Assets (the red T, the colourway, the Scottish codes) stay intact so Protect-audience recognition is not disturbed. The positioning shift is in meaning, not identity, and the platform is built to be authentic rather than performative, a distinction the audit flags as the failure mode to avoid.

Product Launch · Go-to-Market

Rubicon Fru-T: 'Out with the old, in with the cold', a Gen Z launch into sparkling iced tea.

A ten-slide, board-facing launch plan for a new 100% natural sparkling green tea under the Rubicon masterbrand, targeting a £5.8M Year 1 RSP and a Gen Z health-and-flavour occasion the parent portfolio doesn't currently own.

ClientAG Barr
BrandRubicon Fru-T
ScopeEnd-to-end GTM plan
Target launchSeptember 2025
01 Context

A clean innovation slot inside AG Barr's portfolio.

AG Barr's Commercial Director set a clear innovation mandate: launch a soft drink aimed at Gen Z (16 to 28), provided it carried a differentiated position against the existing AG Barr stable (Irn-Bru, Rubicon, Strathmore).

The brief was commercial, not creative. The deliverable was a board-level launch plan covering consumer, product, packaging, customer, shopper, supply and financial propositions, ten slides designed to be read by people who approve or reject capital expenditure on innovation.

The work sat on top of an AG Barr-provided dataset plus secondary research from Mintel, Euromonitor and Statista, feeding a full three-year P&L model before any words were written.

02 The Opportunity

Two Gen Z behaviours the portfolio doesn't answer.

Two behaviours sit at the centre of this audience and neither is currently served by an AG Barr brand. 38% of Gen Z actively prioritise health in their soft-drink purchases, and 74% seek interesting flavours when they buy. Traditional RTD tea addresses health but concedes flavour excitement; mainstream CSDs deliver flavour but carry sugar and synthetic baggage.

The growth numbers back the gap. The UK Ready-to-Drink Tea market sits at £86.14M at-home revenue in 2025, growing at a 2.67% CAGR through 2029, with the carbonated RTD tea sub-segment growing 11% year-on-year. Functional soft drinks are moving from niche to aisle-captain territory, with retailers like Tesco now dedicating functional bays (Beeson, 2024).

+11% Carbonated RTD tea category growth (YoY)
38% Of Gen Z prioritise health in soft-drink choice
74% Of Gen Z seek interesting flavours
Insight

Gen Z doesn't want to choose between healthy and exciting. The winning SKU is one that collapses the trade-off, natural, functional, and flavour-led in a format Gen Z actually drinks: sparkling, 500ml, at an impulse price.

03 The Product

Fru-T by Rubicon, sparkling iced green tea with real fruit juice.

The product is a 100% natural sparkling green tea with real fruit juice, zero added sugar and zero sweeteners, launching in a 500ml PET bottle under the Rubicon masterbrand. It sits as a horizontal category extension, Rubicon's exotic-fruit equity transferred sideways into functional tea, pushing the brand beyond its Asian-grocery heartland into mainstream CSD space.

Pricing is built for impulse. £1.49 RSP against a 30p liquid cost and a total COGS of £5.16 per twelve-unit case, giving an invoice contribution of £3.78 per case (42.3%) before trade investment. That leaves genuine headroom for category-average retailer margin while holding the pack at an impulse price-point Gen Z will actually pay.

04 Proposition Stack

Five propositions, one launch plan.

The board deck is structured around five propositions, each resolving a different stakeholder's objection. The structure below walks through them in the order a launch actually needs to clear: shopper first (will anyone buy it), customer next (will anyone list it), then packaging, supply and financials.

01
Consumer

Out with the old, in with the cold.

Gen Z (16 to 28), health-conscious and flavour-curious, primed for a 100% natural, sparkling, zero-sugar tea that doesn't feel like a compromise drink. The campaign line, "out with the old, in with the cold," positions Fru-T as the generational reset: cold-brewed rather than sugary, sparkling rather than dull, flavour-led rather than austere.

Who / What / How
Who: Gen Z, 16 to 28, health-conscious, flavour-curious.
What: 100% natural fizzy soft drink, no added sugar or sweeteners.
How: Out with the old, in with the cold campaign platform.
02
Packaging

Natural cues, Rubicon trust.

Primary packaging is a clear 500ml PET bottle so the natural liquid colour does the heavy lifting. Research on naturalness cues in FMCG packaging (Binninger, 2015) shows transparent packs outperform opaque on perceived authenticity. The Rubicon masterbrand sits top-left of the front label to lend credibility; Fru-T's own wordmark anchors the middle; flavour variant (Mango) lives at the bottom.

Secondary pack is a 12-unit SRP built for frictionless shelf-ready merchandising, designed to drop straight into the retailer's chilled impulse bay without labour cost.

Design system
  • Format: 500ml clear PET, 12-unit SRP
  • Hierarchy: Rubicon masterbrand, then Fru-T, then variant
  • Cues: Liquid-visible, natural palette, script flavour callout
  • Launch variant: Green Tea Mango
03
Customer

A category-average margin the buyer can say yes to.

Customer margin is held at 40% POR, deliberately matched to the category average rather than pushed below it. That's a choice: going higher squeezes the retailer, going lower telegraphs a weak launch. Matching the benchmark removes commercial friction and makes the listing decision purely about demand and differentiation, both of which the consumer and category data already support.

The ask to the buyer is backed by Rubicon's recent double-digit brand growth, a 500ml sparkling format no competitor currently offers, and a launch campaign built to pull Gen Z into the aisle from day one.

04
Shopper

Win the FMOT, convert at SMOT.

The shopper plan is built on the uncomfortable truth that 50-70% of soft-drink purchases are decided in-store, and 70-80% are unplanned. Pre-store advertising matters less than shelf execution, which is where the launch investment concentrates.

At the First Moment of Truth, pack design, POS material and chilled-bay positioning work together to get the bottle noticed in under two seconds. At the Second Moment of Truth, live in-store sampling lets Gen Z actually taste the product, the single highest-converting tactic for a new natural beverage, and the one that closes the "is it worth £1.49" question on the spot.

05
Supply & Logistics

Engineered for AG Barr's existing rails.

The launch is designed around AG Barr's existing operational capability, not new infrastructure. Green tea leaf is sourced through established tea estates and added to AG Barr's existing procurement book; liquid manufacturing sits inside current plant capacity; logistics and chilled distribution mirror the existing Rubicon bottled portfolio.

The upside is speed-to-shelf and lower launch-risk capex; the trade-off is that SKU expansion beyond Green Tea Mango needs capacity planning before it happens. The Year 1 plan explicitly holds to one variant to prove the model before extension.

Operational decisions
  • Sourcing: Green tea leaf via established estates; added to AG Barr's procurement book
  • Production: Inside existing plant capacity, no new line capex
  • Distribution: Mirrors current Rubicon bottled portfolio
  • Year 1 discipline: One variant (Green Tea Mango) to de-risk before extension
05 Commercial

A three-year P&L that earns its Year 1 loss.

The financial plan is deliberately honest about what launch investment costs. Year 1 runs a net contribution loss of £(757,575) after customer, shopper and marketing investment against a 3.9M unit volume and £5.81M RSP. This is the price of listing distribution, in-store sampling, and awareness-building during the launch window.

The model inflects quickly. Investment ratios step down as the brand scales: customer investment drops from 25% of invoice revenue in Y1 to 10% by Y3; shopper investment from 15% to 5%; marketing from 25% to 10%. By Year 2 the launch turns contribution-positive (£380K), and by Year 3 it delivers £2.43M net contribution on £38.87M RSP, a 13.9% net contribution margin on invoice revenue.

06 Risk

The launch risk the plan names up front.

The principal downside is competitor price and promotion response. A listing win at £1.49 against a 40% retailer POR signals a credible challenger, and incumbents in the CSD and RTD tea space have the scale to undercut on price or flood feature promotions. That can compress Fru-T's already-negative Year 1 net contribution and delay the inflection point the model relies on.

How the plan manages it

Differentiation is held at format and proposition rather than price, a 500ml sparkling tea at impulse pricing is structurally hard for incumbents to copy quickly. Year 1 investment concentrates on sampling and FMOT rather than price promotion, so the brand doesn't train the shopper on discounts. The single-SKU launch discipline keeps operational exposure low while the model proves out, and the step-down in trade investment from Y1 to Y3 is built into the base case, not a stretch assumption.
